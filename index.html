Phase 0
Room Preparation
Before class begins
Prepare before students arrive. The environment is part of the practice.
Dim lights to 20–30% brightness — enough to walk safely, not enough to stimulate the nervous system.
Set room temperature warmer than usual (22–24°C / 72–76°F) — students cool down quickly when still.
Play soft ambient or drone music at very low volume, or complete silence. Avoid music with lyrics.
Place blankets, bolsters, and eye pillows near each mat. Have spares available.
A Tibetan singing bowl or chime is excellent for transitions — have it nearby.
Phase 1
Setup & Arrival
5 min · 0:00 – 5:00
Welcome students as they settle. Speak gently from the moment they enter.
Welcome. Please come to lie down on your mat, on your back. Allow your legs to fall slightly apart, arms a little distance from your body, palms facing up toward the ceiling.
If you would like a bolster under your knees, an eye pillow, or a blanket over your body, please make yourself completely comfortable now. This is your space for the next hour.
Take a moment to make any final adjustments. Find the stillness that feels right for your body today.
— Allow 60–90 seconds of silence —
From this point forward, I invite you to remain as still as possible. Falling asleep is perfectly okay — the practice still works at a deep level.
Keep your voice low and warm from the very first word — set the tone immediately.
Avoid rushing. Give the room time to settle from the energy of the day.
If students arrive late, acknowledge them with a quiet gesture, not words.
Remind students to silence phones before beginning.
Phase 2
Breath & Grounding
5 min · 5:00 – 10:00
Move awareness inward, away from external thoughts. Anchor in the body.
Close your eyes gently. Take a deep breath in through your nose… and let it go completely through your mouth with a quiet sigh. Do this once more — breathe in fully… and release.
Now allow your breath to return to its natural, unforced rhythm. You are not controlling anything. Simply noticing the breath as it moves in and out of the body.
Feel the weight of your body sinking downward. Your heels… your calves… the backs of your thighs… your lower back… your shoulder blades… the back of your head. All of it surrendering to the floor beneath you.
The floor is supporting you completely. You don't need to hold anything up. You can let go.
Slow your own breath as you guide — students unconsciously mirror the teacher's rhythm.
Use long, natural pauses (3–5 seconds) between sentences. Let words land.
Say "the body is relaxing" not "relax the body" — describe what is already happening.
Phase 3
Sankalpa
3 min · 10:00 – 13:00
A Sankalpa is a short, positive intention stated in the present tense. Let it arise naturally — not intellectually.
As you rest in this still and quiet space, I invite you to bring to mind your Sankalpa — your heartfelt intention or resolve. Something deeply true to you. Perhaps "I am at peace." Or "I am whole." Or simply "I am enough."
Don't search too hard. Allow your Sankalpa to arise naturally. If nothing comes, simply rest with the feeling of openness.
Once you have your Sankalpa, plant it gently into your mind — three times — with full feeling and conviction. As if it is already true.
— 30–40 seconds of silence —
The seed is planted. Now we move deeper.
Offer examples but encourage students to find their own.
Keep this phase gentle and non-prescriptive.
The Sankalpa is planted twice — the second time (Phase 8) lands even deeper.
Phase 4
Rotation of Consciousness
15 min · 13:00 – 28:00
Name each body part at a steady rhythm — one part every 2–3 seconds. Students mentally touch each part without moving.
Now we will move awareness through the body. Simply follow along with your mind. There is nothing to do — just notice, just be aware.
Right side of the body
Right hand thumb… index finger… middle finger… ring finger… little finger… palm of the right hand… back of the right hand… right wrist… right forearm… right elbow… right upper arm… right shoulder… right armpit… right side of the chest… right side of the waist… right hip… right thigh… right kneecap… right calf… right ankle… right heel… right sole… right big toe… second toe… third toe… fourth toe… fifth toe.
Left side of the body
Left hand thumb… index finger… middle finger… ring finger… little finger… palm of the left hand… back of the left hand… left wrist… left forearm… left elbow… left upper arm… left shoulder… left armpit… left side of the chest… left side of the waist… left hip… left thigh… left kneecap… left calf… left ankle… left heel… left sole… left big toe… second toe… third toe… fourth toe… fifth toe.
Back of the body
Right heel… left heel… right calf… left calf… right knee… left knee… right thigh… left thigh… right buttock… left buttock… lower back… middle back… upper back… right shoulder blade… left shoulder blade… back of the neck… back of the head.
Front of the body
Top of the head… forehead… right temple… left temple… right eyebrow… left eyebrow… the space between the eyebrows… right eye… left eye… right ear… left ear… right cheek… left cheek… the nose… upper lip… lower lip… the chin… the throat… right collarbone… left collarbone… right chest… left chest… centre of the chest… navel… abdomen…
— Pause —
The whole body… the whole body… the whole body resting.
Maintain a steady, metronome-like pace throughout — consistency helps the mind disengage.
Don't pause long between body parts — brief, rhythmic naming is most effective.
If a student moves or adjusts, don't draw attention to it.
A second pass through the rotation, if time allows, deepens the effect significantly.
Phase 5
Breath Counting
7 min · 28:00 – 35:00
Count backwards from 27 — this occupies the conscious mind just enough to prevent chatter while allowing deeper states to emerge.
Now bring your awareness to your breath. Simply observe it. The rise and fall. The natural rhythm of the body breathing itself.
We are going to count backwards from 27, one count per complete breath. Breathe in… breathe out… 27. Breathe in… breathe out… 26. Continue at your own pace.
— 4–5 minutes of near silence —
If your mind wanders, gently return. Come back to the counting. If you lose your place, simply return to 27. There is no failure here — only returning.
— After counting period —
Release the counting. Return simply to watching the breath — rising and falling — like waves on a still ocean.
Reduce your speaking significantly in this phase — the silence is the practice.
Speak only to anchor students if you sense energy rising in the room.
If students fall asleep here, that's perfectly fine and common.
Phase 6
Pairs of Opposites
8 min · 35:00 – 43:00
Rapidly evoke opposite sensations. 20–30 seconds each. Move swiftly — no narrative. Trains the mind to witness without attachment.
We are going to move through pairs of feelings and sensations. Don't analyse them. Simply allow each experience to arise and pass away. You are the witness — not the experience itself.
Feel heaviness… your body is heavy, sinking, dense. Every part of you is very heavy.
Now feel lightness… your body is weightless, floating, airy. Light as feathers.
Feel warmth spreading through your body… pleasant warmth, like sunshine.
Now feel cool… refreshing coolness washing over you.
Feel discomfort… a slight tension, a tightening somewhere in the body.
Now feel ease and pleasure… softness, comfort, relief.
Feel sadness… allow sadness to be present, without pushing it away.
Now feel joy… a lightness in the heart, a smile behind the eyes.
Feel fear… a contraction, a quickening.
Now feel courage… an expansion, an openness in the chest.
Whatever you have felt — you are not any of it. You are the one who witnesses it. Resting always in that quiet centre.
Keep transitions between opposites swift — rapid alternation is what breaks habitual mental patterns.
Your voice should stay even across both pleasant and unpleasant feelings — model neutrality.
This phase can surface emotions for some students. Hold space with calm, grounded presence.
Phase 7
Rapid Visualisation
7 min · 43:00 – 50:00
One image every 5–10 seconds. Students visualise without analysis. Move swiftly — variety matters more than content.
Now allow images to arise in your mind's eye. Simply see each one — clearly and briefly — then let it dissolve. There is nothing to understand, nothing to hold onto.
A candle flame… a calm, still ocean… a snow-capped mountain… a lotus flower opening at dawn… a crow on a bare winter branch… a full moon reflected in a lake… a child running in a field… a great fire burning… a golden key… a red wooden door… a desert at sunrise… a spinning wheel… a deep forest path… an open hand… a flock of birds lifting from a tree… a crescent moon… an ancient stone wall… a waterfall… a single candle… an empty sky.
— 30–60 seconds of silence —
The images dissolve. Only the still, silent witness remains. Rest here.
Mix nature scenes, objects, emotions, symbols — variety prevents the mind from latching on.
Pause 2–3 seconds between each image to allow students time to form the picture.
Avoid images with strong personal or cultural associations that may alienate students.
Phase 8
Sankalpa · Second Planting
2 min · 50:00 – 52:00
At this depth of relaxation, the subconscious mind is highly receptive. The intention lands now like a seed in fertile soil.
Return now to your Sankalpa — your heartfelt intention. Recall it with clarity and feeling.
From this deep and still place, plant your Sankalpa into the soil of your being. Repeat it three times — with full faith, as if it is already completely true.
— 40–50 seconds of silence —
It is done. The seed is planted in fertile ground.
Your voice should be at its softest and slowest here — students are in their most receptive state.
Do not offer example Sankalpas — trust that each student knows their own.
The silence in this phase is as important as the words.
Phase 9
Externalisation
5 min · 52:00 – 57:00
Bring students back slowly and in layers. Moving too quickly can cause disorientation. Inner body first, then senses, then movement.
Begin to become aware of your breath once more. Simply the breath, rising and falling.
Become aware of your physical body — the weight of it, the temperature of your skin, the contact of your body against the mat.
Become aware of the room around you — the temperature of the air… any sounds you notice… the quality of the light behind your closed eyes.
Very slowly, begin to invite gentle movement. Wiggle your fingers… and your toes. Take a slightly deeper breath. Allow a stretch to move through your body if it wishes to.
Slowly, when you are ready, roll onto your right side. Rest here for a moment — there is no hurry.
— Allow 45–60 seconds on the side —
When you feel ready, use your hands to gently press yourself up to a comfortable seated position. Take your time. Let your head come up last.
Never rush this phase — moving too quickly shocks the nervous system out of deep rest.
Ring a singing bowl or soft chime once before beginning the externalisation.
Some students may feel emotional as they emerge — hold space with calm, grounded presence.
Keep your voice gentle but slightly more present — you are calling students back from a deep place.
Phase 10
Closing & Integration
3 min · 57:00 – 60:00
Keep closing words brief and grounding. Allow students to sit in silence before the class formally ends.
As you sit here, take a moment to notice how you feel. You may feel still, spacious, perhaps a little soft and inward. This is the natural state of a rested nervous system. You have done nothing — and yet something has shifted.
Bring your palms together at your heart. Bow your head gently.
Thank you for your practice. May you carry this stillness with you into the rest of your day.
— Ring bowl · Hold silence —
Namaste.
Hold at least one minute of silence after students come to seated — let them integrate.
Avoid immediately launching into announcements — give the space a moment to settle.
Keep the lights low until students begin to pack up.
If students want to sit in silence longer, allow it.

Youth Journey · Phase 0
Youth-Safe Setup
Before session begins
Designed for middle school and high school students. Keep language simple, choice-based, trauma-informed, and non-mystical.
Before we start, you are in charge of your own comfort. You can lie down, sit against a wall, or sit in a chair. Your eyes can be closed, softly open, or looking down.
This is not a test. You do not have to relax perfectly. You are just practicing noticing your body and giving your nervous system a short reset.
Give students options: lying down, seated, eyes open, or eyes closed. Choice helps students feel safe.
Avoid saying students must be still. Say they may adjust quietly if they need to.
For school settings, skip phrases that may feel too spiritual. Use reset, calm, focus, body, breath, and attention.
Youth Journey · Phase 1
Arrival & Choice
2 min · 0:00 – 2:00
Start with control and consent. Students are more likely to engage when they do not feel trapped.
Find a position that feels okay for your body. You can lie down, sit, or lean against something. Let your hands rest somewhere comfortable.
Take one slow breath in through your nose if that feels comfortable… and let it out through your mouth. Again, breathe in… and let it go.
Notice one thing touching the floor or chair. Your feet, your legs, your back, or your hands. Let that contact remind you: I am here. I am supported.
— 20 seconds of silence —
Normalize fidgeting. For some students, tiny movement helps them regulate.
Keep your voice calm but not sleepy. Teens often respond better to grounded than overly soft.
Youth Journey · Phase 2
Body Scan Reset
4 min · 2:00 – 6:00
Short, steady body scan. Use simple body areas instead of long anatomical lists.
Bring your attention to your feet. Notice your toes, the bottoms of your feet, your heels. No need to move them. Just notice.
Notice your legs. Your calves, knees, thighs, and hips. Let the legs be heavy for a moment.
Notice your stomach and chest. If your breath feels tight, that is okay. You do not have to change it. Just notice the body breathing.
Notice your hands. Your fingers, palms, and wrists. Let your hands soften if they want to.
Notice your shoulders, neck, jaw, and face. Let your forehead smooth out just a little. Let your jaw unclench if it feels safe.
Now sense your whole body at once. Feet, legs, stomach, chest, hands, shoulders, face. Your whole body resting here.
If students are giggly, keep going without scolding. Calm leadership usually settles the room.
Use “notice” more than “relax.” Some students cannot relax on command.
Youth Journey · Phase 3
Breath Anchor
3 min · 6:00 – 9:00
Use breath as an anchor, not a demand. Some youth feel anxious when breath is over-controlled.
Now choose one anchor. You can notice your breath, the feeling of your feet, or the feeling of your hands.
If you choose breath, silently count: breathing in, one… breathing out, one. Breathing in, two… breathing out, two. Count up to five, then start again.
If your mind wanders, that is normal. The practice is not staying focused forever. The practice is coming back.
— 60–90 seconds of quiet practice —
Come back to your anchor one more time. Breath, feet, or hands. Simple. Steady. Here.
Offer non-breath anchors for students with anxiety, trauma, asthma, or discomfort with breath focus.
For middle schoolers, make the silence shorter. For high schoolers, you can stretch it slightly longer.
Youth Journey · Phase 4
Calm Place Visual
3 min · 9:00 – 12:00
A neutral visualization that avoids forcing imagination. Students may use memory, imagination, or simply a color.
Imagine a place where your body can feel a little more calm. It can be real or made up. A beach, a room, a field, a car ride, a quiet corner, or even just a color.
Notice what you might see there. Notice what sounds might be there. Notice the temperature. Notice what lets this place feel a little safer or calmer.
In this place, silently say to yourself: I can pause. I can breathe. I can choose my next step.
— 30 seconds of silence —
Do not assume home is safe for every student. Let them choose any place, real or imagined.
The phrase “a little more calm” is realistic and less pressuring than “completely safe.”
Youth Journey · Phase 5
Return & Ready
3 min · 12:00 – 15:00
Return them clearly so they can transition back to class, group, or the school day.
Begin to notice the room again. Notice the sounds around you. Notice the floor, chair, or mat supporting you.
Take a deeper breath in… and let it out. Wiggle your fingers. Wiggle your toes. Gently move your shoulders or neck if that feels good.
Before you open your eyes fully, choose one word for how you want to return: calm, focused, brave, kind, steady, or ready.
When you are ready, open your eyes or lift your gaze. Come back slowly. Take this calmer version of yourself into the next part of your day.
Give students 10–20 seconds before asking them to speak or stand.
Optional closure: students can show one finger for “more calm,” two for “same,” three for “more alert.” Keep it private and low-pressure.
